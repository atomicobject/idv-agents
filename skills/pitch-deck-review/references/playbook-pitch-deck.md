This file contains verbatim quotes the `pitch-deck-review` skill cites when generating output. Quote, do not paraphrase. Every score and red flag traces to a page below.

## §2.1 What a Great Deck Covers (p.6)

> - **Problem & insight:** What is broken, why now, and what non-obvious insight does the team have?
> - **Solution & product:** What you built, how it works, and how it maps to the problem.
> - **Market:** TAM/SAM/SOM with bottoms-up logic. Avoid top-down-only estimates.
> - **Traction:** Revenue, growth rates, retention, key milestones. Show trajectory, not just snapshots.
> - **Business model:** How you make money, unit economics, pricing strategy.
> - **Competition:** Honest positioning. Avoid empty 2x2 matrices. Explain your wedge.
> - **Team:** Why this team wins. Relevant experience, unfair advantages, key hires planned.
> - **Go-to-market:** Current GTM motion and how it scales with this round of funding.
> - **Financials & ask:** How much you are raising, key uses of funds, milestones this capital unlocks.
> - **Vision:** Where this goes in 5–10 years if everything works.

## §2.1 Deck Pitfalls (p.6)

> - Too many slides (aim for 12–18 in the core deck; appendix for supporting detail).
> - Leading with the solution before establishing the problem and insight.
> - Top-down market sizing only ("The global X market is $50B"). Always include bottoms-up.
> - Competition slide that shows no real competitors. Investors know better.
> - No clear articulation of "why now" — what changed that makes this possible today.
> - Financial projections with no clear assumptions or logic behind the growth rates.
> - Asking for a vague amount ("$5–10M"). Be specific about the raise and the plan.

## §1.2 Fundraising Readiness Scorecard (p.4)

> Before entering a raise, founders should honestly assess readiness across five dimensions. Score each 1–5 and discuss gaps with your lead investor or advisor.

| Dimension | What "Ready" Looks Like | Red Flags |
|---|---|---|
| Market & traction | Clear growth trajectory, strong retention, expanding TAM evidence | Flat or declining metrics, unclear ICP |
| Team | Key roles filled, low attrition, clear org plan for next 12–18 months | Critical gaps in leadership, recent departures |
| Financial clarity | Clean financials, clear unit economics, defensible projections | Messy books, no model, unrealistic forecasts |
| Narrative & positioning | Crisp story, clear differentiation, compelling "why now" | Muddled pitch, no clear wedge, reactive positioning |
| Materials & data room | Deck, model, data room 80%+ complete | No deck, no model, no organized diligence materials |

For pitch deck review, the two most relevant rows are **Narrative & positioning** and **Materials & data room**.

## §6.2 Common Objections (p.17–18)

Use these to anticipate "what would investors push back on this slide" when reviewing.

> Identify the 5–7 most likely objections and prepare clear, honest responses for each. Common objections by stage:
> - **Market too small:** Prepare bottoms-up TAM expansion logic and analogies to markets that grew unexpectedly.
> - **Competitive concerns:** Show your wedge, switching costs, or structural advantages. Acknowledge strong competitors honestly.
> - **Team gaps:** Explain your hiring plan with specific profiles and timelines. Show self-awareness about what is missing.
> - **Unit economics:** Walk through the path to profitability at scale. Show which levers improve with growth.
> - **Timing / "why now":** Identify the structural change (technology shift, regulatory change, behavior change) that makes this the right moment.
> - **Capital efficiency:** Demonstrate discipline in spending. Show what you achieved with prior capital relative to peers.

## §7.4 Where AI Helps and Where It Hurts (p.20)

The pitch-deck-review skill must respect the right column.

| AI Should Help With | AI Should NOT Do |
|---|---|
| Drafting initial outreach templates that the founder edits | Sending any message autonomously |
| Summarizing investor backgrounds for personalization | Generating mass-personalized outreach at scale (investors can tell) |
| Suggesting follow-up timing based on engagement signals | Deciding which investors to deprioritize or drop |
| Identifying talking points based on investor interests | Fabricating connections or shared interests that do not exist |
| Formatting and proofreading | Replacing the founder's authentic voice with AI-generated language |

For pitch-deck-review specifically: do not rewrite the founder's deck text in your own voice. Critique and suggest; do not replace.
